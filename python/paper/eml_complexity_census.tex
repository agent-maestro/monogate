\section{EML Complexity Classification}
\label{sec:complexity}

\subsection{Motivation}

The Infinite Zeros Barrier (T01) establishes that $\sin(x) \notin \mathrm{EML}\text{-}k$
for any finite $k$. This raises the complementary question: for functions
that \emph{are} in some finite $\mathrm{EML}\text{-}k$ class, what is the
minimum $k$?  We introduce a computable classification.

\subsection{The SVD Projection Floor}

\begin{definition}[EML-$k$ floor]
For a function $f \in C[a,b]$ and depth $N$, the \emph{EML-$N$ projection floor} is
\[
  F_f(N) = \|f - \Pi_N f\|_2^2,
\]
where $\Pi_N$ is the orthogonal projector (in $L^2[a,b]$) onto the
column span of the $N$-depth EML atom matrix.
\end{definition}

$F_f(N)$ is monotone non-increasing in $N$ (by construction),
computable (SVD of a finite matrix), and equals the minimum MSE
achievable by any linear combination of depth-$\leq N$ EML trees.

\begin{definition}[EML-$k$ class]
$f \in \mathrm{EML}\text{-}k$ if $F_f(k) < 10^{-8}$ and $F_f(k-1) \geq 10^{-8}$.
\end{definition}

\subsection{The N=3 Singularity}

The striking feature of Table~\ref{tab:census} is that all standard
transcendental functions (sin, cos, tanh, erf, Gaussian, $e^{-x}\sin x$,
etc.) are EML-3. None are EML-1 or EML-2 (except polynomials/rationals
and the EML-native $e^x, \ln x$).

The $N=2 \to N=3$ improvement is always several orders of magnitude
larger than the $N=1 \to N=2$ or $N=3 \to N=4$ improvements
(see Table~\ref{tab:census}). We call this the \emph{N=3 singularity}.

\begin{remark}[Schanuel explanation]
By Schanuel's conjecture, depth-3 EML atoms introduce the first
algebraically independent functions (EL-height-3) beyond the depth-1/2
layer.  The jump at $N=3$ is the point where the EML atom dictionary
first spans oscillatory curvature --- the key feature required by
sin, cos, erf, and Gaussian functions.
\end{remark}

\subsection{EML-4 Candidates}

Session S106 identifies functions that remain above $10^{-8}$ at depth 4
with the current 86-atom dictionary. These include:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\sin(\sin x)$ (composition of EML-3 functions)
  \item $x^6, x^7$ (high-degree monomials, slower convergence)
  \item $\sin(x^2)$ (chirp-type function with increasing frequency)
  \item $\exp(\sin x)$ (composition involving exp)
  \item $\sin(\pi x)$ (period mismatch with probe domain)
\end{itemize}

These are EML-4+ \emph{given the atom dictionary at hand}, not necessarily
requiring depth $> 4$ in the limit.  A larger atom dictionary at depth 4
(more shapes, more leaf patterns) may reduce their floors.
The separations $x^6, \sin(\sin x)$ as genuinely needing depth $> 3$
are conjecture pending larger experiments.

\subsection{Implications}

The EML-$k$ classification has three notable properties:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Computable}: given any callable $f$ and domain, the floor
    $F_f(N)$ can be computed in $O(N_\mathrm{atoms} \cdot n_\mathrm{grid})$ time.
  \item \textbf{Continuous}: the full profile $\{F_f(1), F_f(2), \ldots\}$
    is more informative than a single integer label.
  \item \textbf{Novel}: no existing complexity measure for analytic functions
    has this computable, data-driven structure based on a single gate.
\end{enumerate}
